Lingually

Your immersive language learning application

PROBLEM

Language learners are having a hard time consistently practicing new languages.

I noticed my friends and family had a hard time learning new languages. They are initially motivated to learn a new language but slowly quit over time.
This had me wonder: how might we empower users to learn new languages and create a habit of doing so?
Role: UX/UI Designer (solo project for CareerFoundry UX Bootcamp)
Tools: Adobe XD, Marvel, Google Sheets
Timeframe: May 2021 - Jun 2021

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

Lingually transitioned from a vocabulary to a language app.

Before Lingually became a language learning app, it began as a vocabulary app. Though the content was different, the core problem was comparable. I evaluated three existing vocabulary apps to produce a list of likes and dislikes that guided the solution.

USER INTERVIEWS

My interviewees considered learning new vocabulary as vocabulary in new languages.

I interviewed three language and vocabulary learners to gain insight on how they approached learning and the educational tools they preferred. I gathered the interview data by separating my observations into a Doing-Thinking-Feeling chart.
This is when Lingually diverged from a vocab app to a language learning app. All interview participants used language apps over strictly vocabulary apps and focused on their experience with learning new vocab in non-native languages during their interviews.

KEY RESEARCH INSIGHTS

Interviewees were more motivated to study languages for personal reasons and learned better through realistic conversations.

I noticed from the doing/thinking/feeling data that users that began learning languages for personal reasons were more motivated to continue doing so. The interviewees also preferred learning in-person, and had varying study habits.

PROTO-PERSONA

Personifying research data insights.

I created a persona using the research to give a face for users with needs that Lingually aimed toward solving. When designing Lingually, it was helpful to think back on how my solutions helped solved the persona's specific problem.

USER STORIES

Pinpointing 3 specific problems to solve.

I drafted user stories to cater the solution toward specific problems that were unique to the persona. This put myself in the persona's perspective and helped narrow down the design scope.

IDEATION + DESIGN

Creating low fidelity wireframes.

I created low fidelity wireframes to address the persona's user stories needs. The low fidelity framework helped me focus on functionality for helping the persona achieve their goals rather than content and UI.

TESTING + ITERATION

Addressing 3 usability errors in the design.

I conducted usability tests to assess whether language learners could complete specific scenarios related to the persona's goals using the app prototype. I organized the test data into a usability test report then continously iterated on the lofi wireframes to improve its usability in order of error severity.

CONCLUSION

Key learnings and takeaways from my first UX project.

Lingually was my first official UX project (woohoo ðŸ¥³)! It was both fun and mind-boggling to see the actual app coming together. There are definitely things I would do differently if I started it all over again, but for now, I'll take the lessons I learned to improve my next project.
  1. Always design with the user in mind. I was initially tasked with designing a vocabulary app, but my interview participants primarily used language apps. Because of this, I pivoted my design to fit toward their needs. Sometimes, assumptions and changes need to be made when lacking "sufficient" research data. It's important to remain objective to the user base
  2. New set of competitive analyses. While working on this case study, I realized that because I pivoted the design scope, I should have performed a new set of competitive analyses focusing on language solutions. This would have helped me understand any gaps in that market and spark ideas for Lingually's design.
  3. No idea is perfect. I spent too much time making "pixel-perfect" lofi wireframes using Adobe XD. Next time, I will focus on ideating with pencil and paper.
  4. Testing lo-fi wireframes. I noticed during usability tests that the lack of content and color in my lo-fi prototype distracted users from completing tasks. In the future, I wouldn't test my wireframes until they were more fleshed out so that I can gain feedback on functionality rather than small details.
