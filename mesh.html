Mesh

Your early tech event networking hub

PROBLEM

Early tech talent are not getting traction through traditional job application methods

It is difficult for early PM, designer, and developer applicants to stand out. The roles they apply for often have 100+ applications. Eventually, most receive a templated rejection email.
This problem is close to our hearts as everyone in our team has persevered through this challenge. We created Mesh to help tech talent explore a more effective approach to launch their careers.
Role: Product Designer, Front End Dev (COLAB10 Product Team)
Team: Seb Agudelo (PM), Taylor Reichner (Dev)
Tools: Figma, VSCode, GitHub, Miro
Timeframe: Mar 2022 - May 2022

RESEARCH

Candidates that focused on networking built relationships that helped launch their career

We developed several assumptions revolving around the job search process that may have been hindering applicants. We then interviewed six participants with questions revolving around:
  1. Participants' overall experience in job searching
  2. Opinions on resume/CV and cover letter writing
  3. Motivation during job hunt
Our research showed that participants didn't need new solutions for our assumptions. We also noticed that participants that took the extra effort in reaching out to recruiters, hiring managers, and existing employees were more successful in landing interviews.

IDEATION

Helping tech talent expand their professional network through industry events

We brainstormed how to help applicants network beyond cold outreach and landed on helping them explore industry events. The idea was that through events, early applicants could more easily connect with industry professionals. They can then track who they've met and build lasting relationships.
We used an impact vs effort matrix to prioritize features for our MVP. Our developer assessed feature effort based on technical feasibility, while our PM and I rated impact off our user-base knowledge.

DESIGN

Pivoting the MVP to meet time constraints during wireframing

After narrowing our scope, I begain creating user flows and lo-fi wireframes for our MVP. However, shortly after a change in our dev team, we found our original MVP was not feasible. We got together as a team to discuss features to backlog and agreed to pivot from a native iOS app to a responsive web app.
Meanwhile, I had already started on mobile hi-fi wireframes. I made the call to the team to re-hash our current designs as mobile web designs and delay designing for larger screens so we could meet time-constraints.

TESTING + ITERATION

Improving the design through preference and usability testing

We started with a preference test to assess our visual design. Then, we conducted usability tests to understand whether users could navigate our prototype given scenarios and success criteria. We worked together to synthesize this data as I improved our design in a continuous cycle of iteration.

Learnings + next steps

We hope to expand on the relationship form factor of Mesh

What makes Mesh valuable is its focus on creating professional relationships. Our next steps are to add features that track event contacts and encourage users to stay connected with them.
Mesh is the first product that I created alongside a team. Below are a few examples of what I learned so far -- there is still plenty to learn as our team is eager to continue improving this product!
  1. Cross-Functional Communication
    The dev and I had a few mix ups due to confusion from both sides. I know now the importance of making sure my designs are understood while also understanding dev capabilities.
  2. Pivoting Design Scope
    I learned how to efficiently design pivot due to unforeseen scope changes. This was largely due to the open and communicative environment the team and I had created.
  3. Design Library
    Learning Figma component and library functions were key for keeping our brand consistent and saving design time.
