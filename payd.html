Payd

Your all inclusive e-wallet application

problem

Mobile payment users are having difficulty undertsanding and managing their finances.

The use of mobile payment options is rapidly growing due to the convenience financial applications can provide. However, many financial apps are limited in functionality and don't provide users with a comprehensive financial summary.
The goal of this e-wallet app is to create a single platform where users can send, receive, and budget money so they can better manage their spending habits.

Competitive analysis

To get an idea of how to solve the problem, it helped to explore solutions that were already out there.

I began by researching two existing e-wallet apps: PayPal and Apple Pay. These analyses helped me identify success points and areas of improvements that sparked ideas for Payd's design.
See Competitive Analyses

User interviews

Successful solutions ultimately depend on user needs and goals.

Therefore, I interviewed e-wallet app users with the following research goals in mind: Vital Features, Contexts of Use, and Pain Points.
I grouped the interview data to form affinity maps, which provided a visual understanding of user thoughts feelings, and needs surrounding e-wallet apps.

User personas

All users have their own specific needs and goals, Payd's users are no exception.

Using the affinity maps, I molded user personas to represent e-wallet users with needs, goals, and frustrations that Payd was to aim to solve.
These personas gave identities to Payd's users - making sure it's useful for them became that much more personal.

Journey maps

I then shaped journey maps that depict how my personas might use Payd. Their expectations, thoughts, and feelings should align with those in the maps when working through their scenarios using Payd.

User flows

Every building needs some structure, same goes for Payd.

I created user flows that suggest how my personas might navigate Payd to complete specific tasks. This was a vital step as it influeced Payd's information architecture.

Information architecture

Revisiting existing solutions again, I performed a content audit of the financial management app: Intuit Mint. The audit gave me inspiration on how to structure Payd with the user flows I've already had in mind.
Next, I created an initial site map for Payd. I assessed my map with an online card-sort, then updated it to better match my users' expectations for the structure of an e-wallet app.
With the site architecture draft completed, I was ready to begin sketching mockups.

Low fidelity

I began sketching lo-fi wireframes using pencil and paper. Quick sketches gave me the creative freedom to toss in ideas without committing to one solution or getting bogged down in the details.

Medium fidelity

Once I sketched what I felt would be a viable framework for Payd, I used Balsamiq to create mid-fi wireframes. These wireframes better articulate the solutions and structure I formed in lo-fi.

High fidelity

With the basic functionality planned, it was time to add details. I used Adobe XD to mock up hi-fi wireframes. I pieced these wireframes together to form a prototype and gather feedback on Payd's usability.

Prototype testing ðŸ§©

Usability testing

What might be good solutions in my mind might not be for the user.

I tested the prototype's usability by observing how e-wallet users navigated through it to complete the following specific scenario tasks: Creating a split payment, Adding a payment method, Comparing monthly spending
Again, I used affinity maps to compile the usability test data.

Rainbow spreadsheet

With the affinity map data, I created a rainbow spreadsheet to present user feedback and prevalence. Importantly, it helped identify "user errors" and their severity. Errors were noted when test participants did not meet scenario success criteria.
The errors uncovered Payd functions that needed improvement and the spreadsheet helped determine which were a priority to fix based on impact and effort.
See Rainbow Spreadsheet

Design refinement ðŸ”¨

error addressal

Using the data I gathered with the rainbow spreadsheet, I addressed each error in order of priority and iterated on my high fidelity wireframes. The new solutions were based on my understanding of user feedback and the data compiled so far.
Issue: Users had trouble completing Scenario #3 of comparing monthly spending.
Severity: High
Evidence: Four of six test participants were confused with how the comparison and current buttons worked. The other two participants completed the task but only after much experimentation.
Solution: Remove comparison and current buttons as they didn't provide users with vital information regarding their spending habits.
Additional Updates: Update tab and pie chart UI and implement functionality to add categories.
See More Errors

Visual design ðŸŒˆ

Visual design principles

Tapping into emotional design and Gestalt's laws of Similarity and Emphasis, Payd transitioned from grey scale to a gradient of colors.
Following the laws of Proximity and Good Continuation, I used a "card" design to separate sections within the app.

iOS human guidelines

Adhering to the iOS Clear Path guideline, I divided the Expense tab into two. This provided a clearer path for users to complete tasks (ie. Scenario #3) and still aligns with the Bar guideline of 3-5 icons per navigation bar.
I also readjusted icon sizes to match those of the Icon guideline.

Designing for accessibility

Guided by the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) of Show Location, I added a Home tab and moved the Cards tab to the Settings so users will better understand where they are within the app.
I also included more color, contrast, and shadowing for increased Visibility and Understandability.

The takeaways ðŸ¤”

Challenges

  • User interviews

    The specificity and clarity of interview questions are important, both for the sake of the collected data and the participants volunteering their time to help. Some interview questions were confusing - I could have worded them better.

  • Design with personas in mind

    The personas were molded to fit my user groups well. However, I found as I focused on the little details, I lost sight of my personas at times.

  • Done is better than perfect

    This motto is a key takeaway for me following this project. I got a bit lost in the sauce tweaking minor details, so much so I ended up with five hi-fi wireframes. I've learnt to tone it back some, and that perfecting the visual design is not a priority for early hi-fi.

  • Human Guidelines

    I did not review iOS human and accessibility guidelines until late in the project process. Reviewing these guidelines earlier would have led to fewer iterations (therefore time and money saved) as I made a few key changes to the project later in the design process.

next steps

  • User testing

    Overall, users were excited to follow Payd's progress and felt the changes made throughout the UX process improved the app's usability. My next steps would be another usability test to present the further improvements I've made and identify whether I need to make more.
