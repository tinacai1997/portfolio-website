Payd

E-WALLET APPLICATION

OVERVIEW

Mobile payment users are having difficulty undertsanding and managing their finances.

The goal of this e-wallet app is to create a single platform where users can send, receive, and budget money so they can better manage their spending habits.

ROLE

UX / UI designer
End-to-end design

TOOLS

Figma, Adobe XD, Balsamiq, MS Excel

TIMEFRAME

Jun 2021 - Oct 2021

Understanding the problem

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

I began my research by performing competitive analyses on two popular, existing e-wallet apps: PayPal and Apple Pay. These analyses identified success points and areas of improvements that guided the beginnings of Payd's design.

USER INTERVIEWS

I then conducted user interviews with our intended user groups with the following research goals in mind:

Identify tasks users would like to complete using an e-wallet app.

Collect data on the context in which users would use an e-wallet app.

Document user highlights and pain points with existing e-wallet apps in the market.

I grouped the data from these interviews to form affinity maps. The affinity maps provided a good visual understanding of recurring e-wallet user thoughts and feelings, which provided a foundation for the problems Payd needs to solve.

Empathizing with users

USER PERSONAS

I used the themes identified through the affinity maps to mold two user personas that represented potential users with needs, goals, and frustrations that Payd was to aim to solve. These personas provided a backbone to focus on throughout the whole project; they gave faces to the users I needed to empathize with. The goal now was to create an e-wallet app that would meet their needs.

USER JOURNEY MAPS

Using my user personas, I shaped user journey maps that provided examples how my personas might use my e-wallet app. The expectations, thoughts, and feelings of Payd's potential users should align with those in these journeys.

Creating the foundations

USER FLOWS

The research and analysis I've conducted up to this point paved way into defining several core functions for Payd. I used this information to create three user flows that suggest how my personas would complete three specific tasks using Payd's core functions.

INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

Before laying out Payd's information architecture, I performed a content audit of the existing financial management app, Intuit: Mint.
Using the content audit data, I created an initial site map for Payd. To assess the understandability of my site map, I conducted an online card-sort to compare my map and page groupings with what my intended users would expect. I used this data to update my site map to provide an information architecture that better matched my users' expectations for the structure of an e-wallet app. This post card-sort site map provided a foundation for the screen structure that I would follow when wireframing.

Wiraming the solution

LOW FIDELITY

With the core functions and information architecture I had developed in hand, I began sketching lo-fi wireframes for Payd. In these wireframes, I had actually sketched the expense core function, rather than the login/sign up function identified in my user flows. Perhaps sketching the expense functions without considering its user flows and scenarios were what led to some complications later down the road- but we'll get to that soon. Nevertheless, creating these lo-fi wireframes allowed me to play around with ideas before committing to a specific solution.

MID FIDELITY

I used Balsamiq to refine the functions of Payd through mid-fi wireframes. With these wireframes, I was able to better articulate my ideas for solving my user's needs and goals. This step gave structure and content to the initial thoughts I produced during lo-fi.

HIGH FIDELITY

I used Adobe XD to create my first iteration of hi-fi wireframes. These wireframes were the starting point of the UI and formal structure of Payd. Before spending too much further time on visual aspects, I connected these wireframes to create an interactable prototype. This prototype was used to gather feedback on whether my solutions were useful and usable through usability testing.

Testing the solution

USABILITY TESTING

I conducted usability tests with our intended user groups to assess the usability of Payd's core functions through the following scenarios:

Request a split payment.

Add a payment method.

Compare monthly spending between two months.

During sessions, I observed how participant's navigated through the app and recorded my observations. I then used affinity maps again to compile this data into categories.

RAINBOW SPREADSHEET

Using the data grouped in my second set of affinity maps, I created a rainbow spreadsheet to provide a different perspective on the affinity map data. Importantly, the rainbow spreadsheet identified critical errors and rated their severity based on recurrence and purpose of function.

The spreadsheet uncovered several important errors to address. Notably, most participants had trouble with scenario #3 (as I alluded to before). Users felt there were too many elements within those screens and most had unclear purposes. Additionally, most participants could not identify certain visual elements, like the sliding window. Given this list of errors, I was able to prioritize which to address based on impact and effort to create the next hi-fi wireframe iteration.

Refining the design

VISUAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES

After addressing feedback from my usability tests and iterating on my design, I started the next step in my iteration process: visual design. This step involved reviewing Gestalt's design principles and emotional design to convert Payd from grey scale, to a gradient of color and structure. These visual decisions continually built upon as I improved the UI of Payd.

iOS HUMAN GUIDELINES

I studied the iOS human guidelines to give further direction on the structure of the elements in Payd. A significant change I made following this review was Payd's navigation. The clear path guideline indicated that the pathways to tasks be fast and easy to get to. This iteration is where I ultimately decided to separate the two sub-tabs within the Expense tab into two main tabs of their own. I think this change was needed as it certainly helped declutter the confusion from scenario #3 from usability testing.

DESIGNING FOR ACCESSIBILITY

I further focused on improving the UI and functionality of my app by catering to users with various disabilities. These improvements were based on my review of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) and good accessible designs. Importantly, the Show Location guideline played a crucial role in updating my tab bar yet again. The home tab was added and the cards tab removed to help users understand where they are within Payd.

Future proofing the design

STYLE GUIDE & DESIGN LANGUAGE

To keep Payd's design consistent for any future updates and improvements, I put together a style guide and design language system that dictates its UI, tone, and feel. This is a resource for myself and others to look back to when iterating Payd.
