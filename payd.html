Payd

Your all inclusive e-wallet application

Overview

Payd is an e-wallet app that helps its users manage their finances. Like a real wallet, users can conveniently send, receive, and store money using Payd. Users can also budget and track their spending.
Payd provides a single platform for all financial needs, making it the perfect app for big purchases or just a quick coffee on the go.
Role: UX/UI Designer (solo)
Tools: Adobe XD, Balsamiq, Google Sheets
Timeframe: Jun 2021 - Oct 2021

Understanding the problem üß†

Competitive analysis

I began by researching two existing e-wallet apps: PayPal and Apple Pay. These analyses helped me identify success points and areas of improvements that sparked ideas for Payd's design. My key takeways were that both apps:
  • Value creating a strong sense of trust and inclusion.
  • Aim to impact society by creating products that improve financial health.
  • Are widely used and marketed through notoriety and compatibility.
See Competitive Analyses

User interviews

I conducted three user interviews that focused on the following research goals: vital features, contexts of use, and pain points.
I grouped the interview data using affinity maps, which provided a visual understanding of user thoughts and feelings around e-wallet apps. With this understanding, I determined my users' main financial problems that Payd needed to solve.

Problems & Goal

From my research, I uncovered that the overarching problems users faced when managing finances were:
  • Users prefer quick, digital payments over handling cash
  • User need a way to track their spending habits and history
  • Users don't like managing multiple cards and finance apps
  • User have preferred go-to finance apps for completing various tasks
With these insights in mind, my main goal when designing Payd became providing a single, exceptionally simple platform for users to send, receive, budget, and track their money.

Empathizing with users üíû

User personas

I created user personas to represent users with the needs, goals, and frustrations I identified during my research. These personas gave identities to my users; I was now aiming to solve the problems of Ricky and Chelsea, rather than e-wallet users in general.

Journey maps

I shaped journey maps to illustrate relatable stories for how my personas might use Payd. The goal here was that their thoughts, expectations, and feelings aligned with those in their respective journeys when using the app.

Structuring the solution üå±

User flows

Based on my personas and journey maps, I pinpointed three core functions to help users accomplish their goals: Account Creation, Payment Methods , and Send/Recieve Payments.
I then created user flows that suggested how my personas might navigate Payd to complete specific tasks using these core functions. This step was important as it influeced the app's information architecture and provided a focus on functionality.

Information architecture

Utilizing my user flows, I created an initial site map. Afterwards, I assessed my map using an online card-sort then updated it to better match user expectations for an e-wallet app's structure. After usability testing and design refinement (explained later), I developed a final site map.

Wireframing the solution ‚ú®

Low fidelity

I sketched lo-fi wireframes for three core functions in pencil and paper. Sketching gave me the creative freedom to ideate without focusing on one solution or getting bogged down in the details.
Note that I had swapped the account creation functionality with comparing monthly spending. You will see that sketching this function without a user flow led to an inconcise design later down the line.

Medium fidelity

After I had sketched what I felt was a viable framework for Payd, I used Balsamiq to create mid-fi wireframes. These wireframes better articulated the solutions and structure I formed during lo-fi.

High fidelity

With the basic functionality and structure of my app planned, it was time to add in details. I used Adobe XD to develop hi-fi wireframes then pieced them together to form an interactable prototype to use during usability testing.

Testing the prototype üß™

Usability testing

Using my interactive prototype, I tested the app's usability by observing how e-wallet users navigated through it to complete the following tasks: creating a split payment, adding a payment method, and comparing monthly spending.
I grouped the test data using affinity maps, then organized a rainbow spreadsheet to create a quantitative representation of the data. The spreadsheet tracked user feedback, error, and severity, uncovering functions that needed improvement and their priority based on impact and effort.
See Rainbow Spreadsheet

Error addressal

I addressed each error in order of priority and iterated on my high fidelity wireframes. The new solutions were based on my understanding of user feedback and the data compiled so far.
Issue: Users had trouble with test scenario #3 of comparing monthly spending.
Severity: High
Evidence: Four of six participants were confused with how the comparison and current buttons worked. The other two participants completed the task but only after much experimentation.
Solution: Remove comparison and current buttons.
Additional Updates: Update tab and pie chart UI and implement functionality to add categories.
See More Error Addressals

Adding visual design üåà

Visual design principles

Tapping into emotional design and Gestalt's laws of Similarity and Emphasis, Payd transitioned from grey scale to a gradient of colors.
Following the laws of Proximity and Good Continuation, I used a "card" design to separate sections within the app.

iOS human guidelines

Adhering to the iOS Clear Path guideline, I divided the Expense tab into two. This provided a clearer path for users to complete tasks (ie. Scenario #3) and still aligns with the Bar guideline of 3-5 icons per navigation bar.
I also readjusted icon sizes to match those of the Icon guideline.

Designing for accessibility

Guided by the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) of Show Location, I added a Home tab and moved the Cards tab to the Settings so users will better understand where they are within the app.
I also included more color, contrast, and shadowing for increased Visibility and Understandability.

Reflecting on my work ü§î

Challenges

  1. User interviews: Interview question clarity is very important, both for the sake of data and the participants' time. Some questions were unclear to participants. I was able to adjust these for later interviews, but next time, I will focus on asking specific, open, and non-leading questions.

  2. Function without flow: When creating lo-fi wireframes, I sketched the compareing monthly spending core function without having a user flow for it beforehand. This ultimately led to confusion during usability testing due to an incoherent design. In the future, I will remind myself to continuously look back at my research and analysis when designing functionality.

  3. Done is better than perfect: I got a bit lost in the sauce while tweaking minor details. I've learned that perfecting the visual design is not a priority for early hi-fi.

  4. Visual design guidelines: The steps I took during the project process followed a UX course. The course had me review iOS human and accessibility guidelines very late in the game. I believe if I was aware of these guidelines earlier, I could have structured my app more accessibly early on. In my next project, I will have a better understanding of these guidelines.

Next steps

Overall, users were excited to follow Payd's progress and felt the changes made throughout the UX process improved the app's usability. My next step would be:

  1. User testing: Another round of usability tests to assess the improvements I've made and identify whether I need to make more.

  2. Engineering collaboration: Begin working with software engineers to bring Payd to life.

  3. Launch Payd: Market the app and launch it into mobile app stores.
