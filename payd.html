Payd

Your all inclusive e-wallet
application.

THE PROBLEM

Mobile payment users are having difficulty undertsanding and managing their finances.
The goal of this e-wallet app is to create a single platform where users can send, receive, and budget money so they can better manage their spending habits.

ROLE

UX / UI Designer
Project Manager

TOOLS

TIMEFRAME

Jun 2021 - Oct 2021

Understanding the problem

Competitive analysis

To get an idea of how to solve the problem, it helped to explore solutions that were already out there.
I began by researching two existing e-wallet apps: PayPal and Apple Pay. These analyses helped me identify success points and areas of improvements that sparked ideas for Payd's design.

User interviews

Successful solutions to the problem ultimately depend on the user. Therefore, I conducted user interviews with e-wallet app users with the following research goals in mind: Vital Features, Contexts of Use, and Pain Points.
I grouped the user interview data to form affinity maps, which provided insight and a visual understanding of e-wallet user thoughts feelings, and needs.
Interviewees consider transaction history to be a vital feature.
Interviewees mainly use e-wallet apps to send money to friends.
Interviewees prefer to not make cash payments.

Empathizing with users

User personas

All users have their own specific needs goals, Payd's users are no exception.
Gathering insight from the affinity maps, I molded user personas that represented e-wallet users with needs, goals, and frustrations that Payd was to aim to solve.
These personas gave faces and identities to the users I was designing for - making sure Payd worked for them became that much more personal.

Journey maps

I shaped journey maps for my personas that depict how they might use Payd. The expectations, thoughts, and feelings of my personas should align with these journeys when working through their scenarios using Payd.

Building the structure

User flows

I created user flows that suggested how my personas might use Payd. This gave me an understanding of how users would approach navigating the app to complete their tasks.

Information architecture

Revisiting existing solutions again, I performed a content audit of financial management app: Intuit Mint.
With this content audit, I created an initial site map for Payd. I assessed my site map with an online card-sort, and updated it to better match my user's expectations for the structure of an e-wallet app.

Wireframing the solution

Low fidelity

With an understanding of Payd's information architecture in hand, I began sketching wireframes. Sketching with pencil and paper allowed me to play around with ideas before committing to a specific solution or getting bogged down in the details.

Medium fidelity

Once I had a better idea of what I felt would be a viable framework for Payd, I used Balsamiq to create mid-fi wireframes. These wireframes better articulated the solutions and structure I initially formulated during lo-fi.

High fidelity

With the basic functionality planned out, it was time to add in some UI. I used Adobe XD to mock up hi-fi wireframes that formed the starting point of Payd's UI. I connected these wireframes to create an interactable prototype, which I used to gather feedback on Payd's usability.

Testing the prototype

Usability testing

What might be good solutions in my mind might not be for the user.
I assessed the usability of the Payd prototype by observing how e-wallet users might navigate through the app and measured whether its functions were useful and usable.
Participants performed the following tasks: Create split payment, Add payment method, Comparing monthly spending. Again, I used affinity maps to compile recurring themes from the usability test.

Rainbow spreadsheet

Using the affinity map data, I created a rainbow spreadsheet to present observations and importantly, identify user "errors" and prevalence. These errors uncovered unsuccessful functions within the Payd prototype.
Notably, task #3 of Comparing monthly spending was confusing to use for most users due to the abundance of visual elements within the expense tab screens.
Given this list of errors, I brainstormed possible solutions, and prioritized which to address first based on impact and effort. As a solo project, I was able to address all errors big and small, however, this was good practice for when I will need to prioritize work under a timeline for team projects. Next, I iterated on my hi-fi wireframes.

Refining the design

Visual design principles

After resolving the errors identified in the rainbow spreadsheet, I began refining Payd's visual design. Utilizing Gestalt's design principles and emotional design, Payd transitioned from grey scale to a gradient of color and structure.

iOS human guidelines

Making my design unique is what sets me apart from competitors. However, it's important to understand and stick to what works. I studied iOS human guidelines to improve Payd's understandability.
Through this review, I made a significant change to Payd's navigation to adhere to the "Clear Path" guideline. I decided to separate the Expense tab's sub-tabs into their own individual tabs. This change provided a clearer path for users to complete their tasks, and certainly further decluttered the confusion from usability test task #3.

Designing for accessibility

Making products and services usable for all is the essence of UX design. I improved on Payd's accessibility to cater to users with disabilities by studying the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG). The "Show Location" guideline led me to update the navigation bar again by adding a Home tab and removing the Cards tab. This change should help users understand where they are within the app. Below is the new and improved site map.

Future proofing the design

Style guide & Design Language

Payd's journey isn't yet over. To keep Payd's design consistent for future updates and improvements, I put together a style guide and design language system that dicates the UI, tone, and feel of Payd.

Interactable Prototype

And with all that research, analysis, and iterations, comes my "final" prototype.
Ideally, my next steps would be to perform another session of usability tests to assess the updates I've made since the last. For now, this is Payd's current prototype.
Iteration Overview

Key takeaways

  • User testing earlier

    I made a lot of iterations on my hi-fi wireframes that I feel could and should have come earlier. If I had performed usability tests on mid-fi wireframes, I could have focused on testing functionality alone, rather than focus on hi-fi details that ended up getting scrapped anyway.

  • Avoid tunneling on details

    I learned during this project that done is better than perfect.

  • Design with personas in mind

    My personas were defined as a reference to look back to and aim to solve problems for. I found as I focused on the small details, I lost sight of my personas at times.

  • Understandable interview questions

    I found during my user interviews and usability tests that the specificity and clarity of my questions were incredibly important, both for the sake of the data I collect and my participants that are volunteering their time to help me. There were some questions that I could have worded better.

  • Review guidelines earlier

    I didn't touch on the iOS human or accessibility guidelines until late in the game. In my future projects, I'd work toward putting accessible and recognizable designs in the forefront of the design process.
