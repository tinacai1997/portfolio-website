Payd

Your all inclusive e-wallet application

PROBLEM

Digital wallet users face an overabundance of finance apps in their mobile phones.

Online sales and transactions have steadily increased over the last decade. The COVID pandemic had increased this rate even further, resulting in more and more apps designed to manage finances.
This led to the question: why do users need to handle so many different apps in order to understand their finances?
Role: UX/UI Designer (solo)
Tools: Adobe XD, Balsamiq, Google Sheets
Timeframe: Jun 2021 - Oct 2021

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

The competition did NOT tell the whole financial story.

I analyzed three popular apps that revolve around managing finances. I learned that though each app does their particular functions very well, none of them provide their users a comprehensive understanding of their finances. Just one of these apps wasn't enough for understanding the entire financial summary.

USER INTERVIEWS

My interviewees prioritized convenience and tracking for their financial transactions.

I conducted user interviews with three financial management app users to understand what they valued most in the apps they use to handle their finances. My research goals were to gauge vital features, contexts of use, and pain points surrounding competitor apps. I then organized the data using affinity maps to uncover any trends.

KEY RESEARCH INSIGHTS

Interviewees wanted basic payment app functionalities, ON TOP of an overview of all their spending.

Based on the groupings from my affinity maps, I noticed interviewees used finance apps on an as-needed basis and each prefer different apps for accomplishing specific tasks. They also expressed the need to improve their spending habits, but had no way of viewing the bigger picture.

PERSONAS + JOURNEY MAPS

Synthesizing research data into personable goals and scenarios.

I created personas to personify the research data and help me understand who would benefit from the solution I was working toward. I also shaped journey maps to illustrate relatable scenarios for each persona. Going through their journeys step by step helped me brainstorm pain points and opportunities for the solution.

IDEATION

3 core user flows to address primary user goals.

I identified three main functionalities to focus on for the solution that catered to my users' financial app needs. I used the opportunities I brainstormed during journey mapping to sketch user flows and features for the core functions.

DESIGN

Wireframing a comprehensive financial management mobile app.

Though I was tasked with creating a web app, I felt a mobile solution was the right approach in terms of both familiarity and simplicity. Mobile financial apps were used more often by my interviewees and allowed me to focus on functionality. I fleshed out the sketches by creating mid fidelity wireframes. Once I felt the functionality was moving toward the right direction, I created a high fidelity prototype for usability testing.

TESTING + ITERATION

Making 3 major improvements to the design.

I conducted usability tests to observe how six financial management app users navigated through the prototype. I grouped the data using affinity maps and organized trends using a rainbow spreadsheet to prioritize usability errors concerning app functionality. I continuously iterated on the design based on test participant feedback and visual and accessibility guidelines to address these errors.

Conclusion

What were my key learnings and takeaways.

Payd was the first UX project that I worked on from concept to completion! It definitely isn't perfect, but was a great place for me to start. In addition to many UX methodologies, I learned many important lessons that I'll take with me to my next project ðŸ˜Š
  1. Research vs. interview questions. While working on this project, I learned the difference between research and interview questions. I know now that when gathering qualitative information, it's important to keep interview questions open ended and non-leading.
  2. Done is better than perfect - keep on iterating. I got a bit lost in the sauce while tweaking minor details. I've learned that perfecting the visual design is not a priority for early hi-fi. I should have turned in minor iterations more frequently, rather than making bulk changes. This would have allowed me to focus on functionality and keep from being overwhelmed.
  3. Insight vs. process driven design. I realized while working on this case study that showing what was pertinent to the problem space was much more valuable than completing and showing off processes. Moving forward, I have a better understanding of what processes to use, when to use them, and how they deliver insights that affect the bigger picture - rather than doing them just to do them.
